## Design Context

### Users
Rondo is for football and futsal players, local organizers, and small communities coordinating real matches on mobile. Players use it to find games, inspect match details, see who is playing, join or reserve a spot, pay, chat, and show up ready. Organizers use it to create games, manage rosters, collect payments, broadcast updates, and run match operations.

The main usage context is fast, phone-first, and practical: users may be checking games between errands, before training, while commuting, or near the pitch. The interface should make discovery, trust, payment, and match logistics feel immediate and low-friction.

### Brand Personality
Clean, intuitive, premium, street-smart, and cutting-edge.

Rondo should feel like a high-end sports product with the accessibility of a local street football community. It can borrow the confidence and polish of Nike, Adidas, Puma running apps, Emirates FreeKick, Grintafy, Uber's clarity, and selected Street FC community energy, but it should not feel like a betting app.

### Aesthetic Direction
The product should evolve from its current dark mobile sports look into a cleaner, more premium system-led visual language. The current black/yellow direction is a starting point, not a fixed requirement. Future color work should define a deliberate palette that feels athletic, high-end, modern, and trustworthy without relying on generic gradients or neon-dark AI aesthetics.

The interface should feel familiar enough to use instantly, but unfamiliar enough to feel like a distinct sports product. Prioritize sharp hierarchy, confident spacing, precise mobile controls, premium imagery, and motion that feels fast and intentional.

Avoid: betting-style energy, AI slop, bland app templates, generic gradients, outdated UI, broken visual states, cluttered dashboards, and decorative effects that do not support the sports/community experience.

### Design Principles
1. Mobile first means thumb-safe, fast-scanning, and safe-area-aware; every primary action must feel natural on a phone.
2. Premium sports clarity beats decoration; use strong hierarchy, restrained surfaces, and decisive typography instead of visual noise.
3. Community trust must be visible; rosters, organizers, payments, match rules, and updates should feel credible at a glance.
4. Motion should be crisp and useful; use it for feedback, state, and spatial continuity, never as generic flair.
5. The visual system should be distinctive but not strange for its own sake; Rondo should feel intuitive on first use and memorable after.
